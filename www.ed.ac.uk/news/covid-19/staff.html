Staff

General information explaining how the University will support and continue to pay staff during the Coronavirus outbreak, including guidance for those able to work from home.

The following information explains how the University will support staff during the Coronavirus outbreak and School/Nursery closures.  For all staff, it covers self-isolation and practical guidance for those who are able to work from home. It elaborates on our commitment that staff will not suffer any financial detriment due to the action the University needs to take to safeguard student, staff and visitor health and safety. Additional guidance is provided for managers. Staff are strongly encouraged to keep up to date by regularly reading the general information provided on the Covid-19 webpages and this section dedicated to staff and employment matters. 

Pay and benefits

Staff salaries and leave

In light of the additional restrictions on social contact and the ‘lock-down’ imposed by the Scottish Government, i.e. that everyone must stay at home, except for very limited purposes, we have revised our approach to leave and pay. As previously advised, and agreed with the joint trade unions, staff will not experience any financial detriment as a consequence of the actions the University has to take as a consequence of the Coronavirus (Covid-19) outbreak. This means that all staff will be paid as normal.

As the vast majority of staff must now stay at home, with many caring for young/school age children, we no longer require staff to take emergency time off to care for their dependants. Nor do we need staff to report – or managers to record – that they are self-isolating due to a member of their household displaying coronavirus symptoms. We do however want and need to know if staff are themselves unwell.

These Staff FAQs will be revised shortly to reflect these changes. We trust that anyone able to work from home will do so if and when they can, and we do not wish to add to everyone’s burden by unnecessary administration and record-keeping.  

Working from home expenses (NEW)

We recognise that some staff may unavoidably incur costs due to having to stay at home during the Coronavirus (Covid-19) outbreak, including doing as much as they can to work from home. Similarly, there will be costs that some staff will not incur, for example commuting to work. 

For that reason, the University will exceptionally consider meeting the purchase cost of equipment or capability only where it is “wholly, exclusively and necessary” to homeworking and could not be supplied in advance by the University. Agreement to the purchase must have been made in advance by your line manager. 

In order to claim this as a legitimate business expense, you will need to evidence that this equipment is wholly, exclusively and necessary for the claimant’s work purposes. There may be tax implications for the individual if they are not.

Staff can check if they are eligible for tax relief on working from home expenses by answering the questions on the HMRC website.

Parking permits

Current parking permits held by staff will be extended by the length of time that homeworking measures are in place. 

Self-isolation 

Guidance for staff on self-isolation, i.e. staying at home, in line with prevailing public health and government advice to help control the spread of the virus. Guidance for staff on reporting the need to self-isolate, how this will be treated absence wise.     

General information 

We recognise that some staff may already be at home having been advised to self-isolate in line with previous Government and public health advice, i.e. due to having returned from a specified country. If you are, you should continue to self-isolate in line with the medical advice you received.  The below is based on the advice issued on 13th March 2020.

If you or someone in your household has symptoms of coronavirus (Covid-19) – i.e. a new, continuous cough and/or high temperature - you must self-isolate, i.e. stay at home, for:

  • 7 days from when your symptoms started if you live alone, or
  • 7 days if you are the first in your household to develop symptoms, or
  • 14 days if you live with others and you or they have symptoms.

If you are the first in your household to develop symptoms:

  • you need to stay at home for 7 days from the day your symptoms start
  • everyone else in your household needs to stay at home for 14 days (from the day your symptoms start)
  • if someone in your household then develops symptoms
    • they need to self-isolate for 7 days from the onset of their symptoms. This could mean that they have to self-isolate for more than the original 14 days.
    • The 14 day ‘whole household’ isolation does not restart each time a member of the household develops symptoms.   So you don’t need to self-isolate beyond 7 days – unless you continue to have a high temperature. 

For example, you develop symptoms (day one). You have to stay at home for 7 days.  Everyone else has to stay at home for 14 days. If on day 10 your partner develops symptoms, they need to stay at home for 7 days – so in total they will have to self-isolate for 17 days. You don’t need to after day seven. No-one else needs to self-isolate after day 14, unless they too develop symptoms.

If a member of your household is the first to develop symptoms:

  • they need to stay at home for 7 days from the day their symptoms start
  • you and everyone else in your household need to stay at home for 14 days
  • if you then develop symptoms
    • you need to self-isolate for 7 days from the onset of your symptoms. This could mean that you have to self-isolate for more than the original 14 days.
    • The 14 day ‘whole household’ isolation does not restart when you develop symptoms.  

For example, your partner develop symptoms (day one). They have to stay at home for 7 days. You and everyone else in your household has to stay at home for 14 days. If on day 10 you develop symptoms, you need to stay at home for 7 days – so in total you will have to self-isolate for 17 days. No-one else needs to self-isolate after day 14, unless they too develop symptoms.

Further information on when and how to self-isolate is available on the Public Health England website. 

The most common symptoms of coronavirus (Covid-19) are recent onset of:

  • new continuous cough and/or
  • high temperature

You do not need to call NHS 111 to go into self-isolation. If your symptoms worsen during home isolation or are no better after 7 days then use the NHS 111 online coronavirus service. If you have no internet access, you should call NHS 111. For a medical emergency dial 999.

Further information can be found on the UK Government website.

Reporting and payment 

As you must not attend work, you must follow the usual absence reporting procedure for your School/Department. You must call your line manager or the designated contact in your School/Department to report your need to self-isolate, and why, i.e. your entire household has to self-isolate or you have symptoms.  Please make sure you are familiar with the reporting arrangements within your School/Department and have to hand the correct contact details.   

If you are well, i.e. have no symptoms, your manager will agree with you if you can work from home. This will depend on the nature of your role and whether you have the equipment, access and approval to do so.  Working from home for the period of isolation will have no effect on your pay, and a record will be kept that you are self-isolating.

If you are well but unable to work from home for any or all of the period of isolation - your manager will place you on paid leave.  Your absence will not be recorded as sickness and will have no effect on your absence record or entitlement to sick pay.

You must keep in regular contact with your manager, or the designated School/Department contact via email or telephone.  You must let them know if you develop symptoms during household isolation.

If you are self-isolating because you are unwell, your absence will be treated as sick leave, the University’s Absence Management policy will apply and you will receive full pay for the period of absence related to the virus. 

  If you are self-isolating because you are unwell:

  • your absence will be treated as special sick leave
  • you will receive full pay for the period of absence related to the virus
  • your absence will have no effect on your absence record and entitlement to sick pay  
  • As you are advised not to visit your GP, you may not be able to obtain a Fit Note.   In these circumstances, the University will not require you to provide a Fit Note for any coronavirus related absence of 14 days or less.

Additional information if you have an underlying health condition, are pregnant or are over 70

The UK Government has advised that the following groups should take additional measures to keep themselves safe. These groups include those who are:

  • aged 70 or older - regardless of their medical condition
  • under 70 with an underlying health condition (i.e. anyone instructed to get a flu jab as an adult each year on medical grounds) or complex health condition.
  • are pregnant.

If you are over 70, have an underlying health condition or are pregnant, you should follow the UK Government's social distancing protocols and remain at home.  You must let your manager know as soon as you decide to self-isolate.

If you are well, i.e. have no symptoms of coronavirus, your manager will agree with you what work you can fulfil from home. This will depend on the nature of your role and whether you have the equipment, access and approval to do so.  If your current role cannot be done from home, your manager may ask you to fulfil other tasks from home. 

If you are unable to work from home your manager will place you on paid leave.  Your absence will not be recorded as sickness and will have no effect on your absence record or entitlement to sick pay.

 Access to University buildings and information for essential staff

Building access (NEW)

As of 5pm on Friday 27 March, the majority of our University buildings have been secured and closed. Only those identified as ‘essential staff’ will have access – these staff have been contacted directly with further information. In some circumstances, ‘emergency access requests’ will be considered by Head of College or Professional Services Group and Deputy Secretary.

Some of our buildings remain in partial or full operation, but will only be accessible by staff identified as essential.

Essential staff in Estates will continue to ensure the safety of critical systems which will be serviced regularly as required to allow essential staff to continue working in these buildings.

All University libraries have been closed since 23 March, for more information about Library services and support for staff visit the Library Updates page.

Ensuring a safe working environment for essential staff (NEW)

We have put procedures in place to ensure that essential staff accessing University buildings have a safe working environment. Staff should not attempt to come to work unless they have been identified as ‘essential staff’ and have been provided with a University letter of authority.

All identified essential staff must abide by the Government’s instructions. They must not come to work at all if they are in any of the vulnerable categories publicised or have any of the symptoms of Covid-19, no matter how mild.

Essential staff on site will abide by the following procedures to ensure their safety:

  • Rotas of a shorter duration have been introduced to maintain resilient teams
  • A buddy system will be followed as outlined by the University – lone working is not allowed
  • Social/physical distancing must be applied to all work activity, with a 2 metre minimum distance
  • Staff should wash hands frequently and clean regularly touched points such as door handles and light switches
  • All risk assessments for general work and lab work must be reviewed to take account of any changed methods of operating

School/nursery closures 

Guidance for staff with nursery/school age children.

General information

On 18th March, the Scottish Government announced that schools and nurseries would close on Friday 20th March, potentially through to the normal summer break. We realise this will have significant impact for many of our staff and are committed to supporting those who can work from home to do so in as flexible a way as possible. Everyone will continue to be paid. The general guidance below will be updated to reflect decisions by the Scottish Government regarding closures beyond the Easter break. 

Emergency time off for dependants  

While the Emergency Time of for Dependants policy is intended to give you one or two days paid time off work to deal with an emergency and put in place alternative care, during the Coronavirus outbreak it will be extended to cover time off to care for a dependant while schools and nurseries are closed. 

You are not generally expected to work from home while caring for a dependant. However, during the Coronavirus outbreak, your manager will discuss with you whether it is feasible for you to work flexibly from home, in part or in full.   

We appreciate that you will be juggling many demands, including using and possibly sharing your home as a workplace, while simultaneously caring for young children and potentially trying to home-school your children at key times in their schooling. We know it will take some time to adjust to and establish any kind of rhythm and that what works for one member of staff working at home will not work for another. So there are no rules: it will be what works for you and we trust you to work that out and do what you can, when you can.  

If you can work from home at some point in the day, we ask that you do so if you can and that you do what you can to ensure your manager, your team and other key colleagues know when you are likely to be available. We understand that, with other members of the family at home, it may be difficult to predict when you will be available and that your availability to work may change at short notice. 

If it is impossible for you to work from home, either because of your caring responsibilities or the nature of your role, you will be able to take 10 working days paid leave. This will be pro-rated for staff who work part weeks. Thereafter you will be on paid  leave – and be paid as normal. (PLEASE NOTE that this information was updated on 20 March to include “paid special leave”. We apologise that this was mistakenly left out due to the rapidly changing situation.)

Annual leave 

We appreciate that the Coronavirus outbreak will have impacted many holiday plans and that staff will be thinking of cancelling their leave to take at a later date.  However, in line with our Annual Leave Policy, it is important that staff have a regular break from work, even if this work is being done from home.  This is perhaps more critical to everyone’s health and wellbeing during this time of unprecedented and considerable change.  

If you were planning to be on leave during a normal school closure, e.g. over Easter, you should continue to take this from your annual leave entitlement. 

If you have not already booked such time off, you should ideally consider doing so and discuss with your manager what impact, if any, this would have on business critical activities/essential services.    

In the interest of your wellbeing, and our ability to return to on-campus working at some point later in the year, we would expect most staff to have taken 40-60% of their total annual leave entitlement by the end of the current academic year, i.e. by 31 July 2020. 

Remote working i.e. homeworking  

General information on the University’s move to delivering all but essential services off-campus; guidance on working from home and remote teaching.  Information for staff who cannot fulfil their role from home.

General information

To slow the spread of the virus, on 16th March 2020 the UK Government advised the general public to restrict their social contact and stay at home as much as possible. In line with this advice the University decided to move to on-line teaching and assessment with effect from 23rd March and to transition as much as possible of its operations to be undertaken by staff working from home.

In all of our planning, our priority is always to ensure that key services for our students and staff continue to be provided in the most effective way and that you are supported as much as possible.  The health, safety and wellbeing of our students and staff will always be our top priority and will guide our decision making. 

All Schools and Departments have business continuity plans in place which include an assessment of their business critical activities and what work can be done remotely, i.e. from home. This will recognise that there will be instances where some staff do need to come on campus for part of the week, but can work from home for the rest of the time.

We acknowledge that travelling to work might become difficult so will enable essential staff not already able to park on campus to do so. More information on this will follow.

Guidance on homeworking 

The following points outline the current steps that you should follow for remote and homeworking – we will let you know as soon as possible if this guidance changes:

  • We are continuing to follow the UK Government’s latest advice on social distancing which states that if you can work from home, then you should work from home.
  • Some essential services will always be need to be delivered on campus. If you provide essential services which can only be delivered on campus, we are working with line managers to make every effort to reduce pressure in these areas.
  • Options to deliver your role through remote working must be discussed and agreed with your line manager. If remote working is to be considered, please check the advice on the IS website and review it frequently as it will be updated.
  • If you intend to work remotely and connect to your work computer (remote desktop) then please take these simple actions before you leave the office to enable this.
  • We understand that the prospect of teaching on-campus courses online can seem daunting. There is specific advice on remote teaching, including details of training opportunities and how to access help.  This includes information and tools for teaching remotely and guidance on using Blackboard Learn and Media Hopper.
  • You should review the online tips for using collaboration tools or contact the IS helpline if you have any queries about the remote working system.
  • The usual IS alerts website and Twitter feed will continue to publish known service issues. The IS Helpline will continue to provide support remotely. Please use the online contact form or email as your first point of call.
  • If you are already working from home, please ensure that you stay in regular contact with your line manager and colleagues – for work purposes of course, but also to retain regular social contact.  Please take time to read the section on Health and Wellbeing which focusses on working remotely.  There is also a section below on Guidance for Managers on Managing staff Who Work from Home. 
  • If you are about to work from home and have permission to move a monitor or other equipment to facilitate this, then that information must be logged in line with normal local protocols.
  • If managers need to gather information for contacting staff when working remotely, such as mobile phone numbers, they should be clear with staff on how this information will be used, who it will be shared with, how it will be held and not use it for any other purpose.

As this is a rapidly changing situation please check the Covid-19 staff webpage regularly for updates.

Health and wellbeing 

Wellbeing, Advice and Support services for staff

Feeling overwhemed or anxious by the ongoing Coronavirus outbreak is understandable. We appreciate that you may have concerns about how this will impact you or your family and friends.

Ways to Manage Fears & Anxieties:

  • Keep connected. Maintaining social networks, even online, can help maintain a sense of normality, and provide valuable outlets for sharing feelings and relieving stress.
  • Be mindful of assumptions about others. Someone who has a cough or a fever does not necessarily have coronavirus.  Self-awareness is important in not stigmatizing others in our community.
  • Stay healthy. Continue to practise good hygiene measures:
    • Avoid direct hand contact with your eyes, nose and mouth.
    • Wash hands with soap and water or alcohol hand sanitiser, after coughing or sneezing, after going to the toilet, and prior to eating and drinking.
    • When coughing or sneezing, cover your nose and mouth with disposable tissues and disposing of them in nearest waste bin after use, or cough or sneeze into your sleeve.
    • Avoid contact with others who are sick and stay home while sick.

Read the World Health Organisation’s advice on Mental Health Considerations during Covid-19 Outbreak.

  • Staff Counselling Service have lots of advice on managing stress and anxiety online

Looking after your wellbeing when working from home

When people work from home, they run the risk of their home and work lives blurring into one. Associating home with work makes it a lot harder to switch off, and this stops people from relaxing, sleeping and can cause issues with their mental wellbeing.

Information for Guaranteed Minimum Hours staff

Staff on guaranteed minimum hours (GH) contracts will be paid as normal during the Coronavirus outbreak. 

If you need to self-isolate, you will be paid for the hours that you were offered and accepted (or were rostered to work) prior to having to stay away from work. 

You will also be entitled to Emergency Time off for Dependants, and will be paid for the hours you would have worked.

In line with the general guidance on working remotely/working from home, your manager will agree with you if this is at all feasible.  If you can do your work from home, you will be paid for the hours you are asked to work and these will be processed for payment in the normal way. 

In line with the University’s 2019 agreement with UCU, teaching staff will be paid for any training they are required to undertake to move to on-line teaching and assessment.

If you are well but unable to work from home for any reason (i.e. you are self-isolating, your work cannot be done from home, you do not have the equipment to work from home) your manager will consider if, with appropriate training, it would be reasonable to ask you to help with the essential services which must be maintained on campus. 

If it is absolutely impossible for you to undertake any work, remotely or on campus, you will be placed on paid temporary leave.  Your manager will keep this under review as needs will change over the coming months and as we move to recovery.  ​​​​​​

Pay calculation

If you have already been offered and accepted work for several months to come:

  • you will be paid each month in line with these ‘contractual’ hours. 
  • your manager will let you know what administrative arrangements will be put in place to ensure you are paid for the hours you would have worked, for example inputting your hours via e-Time.  

We are currently exploring how best to calculate pay for staff who have yet to be offered work.  We will update this section as soon as we can

Annual leave

We appreciate that the Coronavirus outbreak will have impacted many holiday plans and that staff will be thinking of cancelling their leave to take at a later date.

Given the potential for extended nursery/school closures, if you were planning to be on leave during a normal school closure, e.g. over Easter, you should continue to take this from your annual leave entitlement.

Information for staff with visas due to expire before 31 May 2020 (NEW)

If your visa is due to expire soon, and you plan to apply for an extension to stay in the UK, please do so by following the information about visa extension applications available on the HR Immigration webpages. 

If all of the points below apply to you, your visa will be automatically extended to 31 May 2020:

  • You are in the UK, and
  • Your visa expires between 24 January and 31 May 2020, and
  • You cannot leave the UK because of travel restrictions or self-isolation due to the Coronavirus (Covid-19) outbreak.

Erasmus+  

Advice for those due to undertake an Erasmus+ placement.

Erasmus+

The University has taken the decision to restrict all international travel by University staff and students to “essential travel only” for a minimum of two months.

If you were due to undertake your Erasmus+ placement before 31 May 2020, we advise that you do not proceed with the visit.

If you have booked travel and/or accommodation, please contact your insurance provider for information about claiming against costs already incurred. You will most likely need to provide your Erasmus+ Training Agreement confirming your attendance and a copy of your flights and/or accommodation booking/receipts (with dates of travel and amount paid). If you have University of Edinburgh travel insurance, please consult our insurance webpages for all forms. 

You should also contact your host institution to reclaim any fees paid to them. Any Erasmus funding already issued to you is likely to be reclaimed at a later stage.

It’s important to consider the practicalities for your team working from home.  It may be that staff have caring responsibilities or home environments that will make homeworking impractical.  Discuss the feasibility for working from home with each member of your team and make decisions on an individual basis. Guidance for staff in relation to the University’s response to Coronavirus is publicly available and regularly updated. If there are any questions that you can’t find the answer here contact your HR team.

Plan for home working by designing a simple contingency plan for your team. This should include all the key tasks/projects that are coming up for the next few weeks identifying key contacts including any suppliers, your proposed action and impact of this. It may be that it’s as simple as using the suggestions below to switch meetings onto Skype/Microsoft Teams but thinking about how you could do things in a different way or what you need to prioritise will be really helpful in making sure you can make quick decisions, know when things have happened and keep staff and students up to date about what’s happening.

Best practice for working at home:

Stay in regular contact with your team – Agree how you are going to communicate with your whole team; if not everyone is able to work from home consider a WhatsApp group or phone chain to relay key information.  Start each day with a team huddle using Skype or Teams to check in with how everyone is doing, relay key information and discuss key focus/tasks for the day/week.  Encourage your team to keep talking and checking in with each other as they would in the office.

Consider what is achievable – It may be that not everyone has access to everything that they would in their normal working environment. Establish ground rules with your team and be clear on deadlines and your expectations of them.

 Here are some questions to consider when planning work for the team:

  • What are the most critical tasks that need to continue?
  • Who is best placed to complete them?
  • Are there shorter-term deliverables that can be easily completed remotely?
  • Are there meetings/workshops/seminars that can take place remotely using the tools available?
  • What goals and limits can you ask your team to set themselves?

Support them as they manage their wellbeing - When people work from home, they run the risk of their home and work lives blurring into one. Associating home with work makes it a lot harder to switch off, and this stops people from relaxing, sleeping and can cause issues with their mental health.

To help:

  1. Encourage your team to plan and structure their working day and take regular breaks - without the distractions of the office and regular face to face meetings they may not be getting a break from their screen. The HSE state that short, frequent breaks are more satisfactory than occasional, longer breaks: e.g. a 5-10 minute break after 50-60 minutes continuous screen and/or keyboard work is likely to be better than a 15 minute break every 2 hours. Encourage your team to take their lunch break away from screens.
  2. Encourage members of your team to set up working spaces in their homes. Not everyone has a spare room, but a corner with a desk that only gets used for working can help.
  3. Talk to them about their concerns and what is going on for them personally. They may have additional caring responsibilities that they are needing to juggle whilst working.
  4. Regularly remind your team of the resources and support they can access remotely to support them. University of Edinburgh Wellbeing Resources 
  5. Celebrate successes and thank your team regularly to help them feel appreciated for what they have achieved.

Recruitment

Honouring Job Offers

All job offers (verbal or written) will be honoured. Every effort will be made to ensure the best on-boarding and induction experience for new staff. 

Recruitment Activity prior to Offer

If you are in the process of recruiting but have not yet reached offer stage, i.e. have already advertised the post and are actively long/short listing etc, you are encouraged to seriously consider the implications and practicalities of proceeding with an appointment at this time.  

Options may be to:

  • Pause – communicating with candidates that we will restart the process in 3-4 months.
  • Cancel – if there is the potential that resource needs might be changed as a consequence of the pandemic.
  • Complete appointment – if the appointment is business critical and you have confirmed that with your budget holder.

Recruitment not yet started

If you have a vacancy but have not yet advertised the post, it is likely that the pool of applicants will be significantly depleted, particularly if applicants would need to relocate to Edinburgh.

You should therefore seriously consider delaying what might not prove to be a productive exercise. 

We should however progress business critical appointments that will support recovery post Covid-19.  Line managers and the Main Budget Holders normal approval processes should be followed.   

New recruits and remote induction

With traditional working arrangements rapidly changing due to the impact of the Covid-19 virus here is some guidance on how to support the induction of new staff when working remotely.

  • Speak to the member of staff as soon as possible before their start date to update them on the University’s guidance regarding Covid-19  and to answer any questions and concerns they may have. Discuss and agree how you will communicate with each other and the team.
  • Review the Induction Guidance for Managers to plan a successful induction considering how to adapt best practice for a remote working environment based on their role.
  • If it is possible for the staff member to work from home, review and discuss with them the Guidance for working from home on the Covid-19 webpages
  • Support them to set up and work remotely. They may not be familiar with the tools the University has for keeping in touch, give them plenty of time to get themselves set up and test connectivity.
  • Make them aware of the Digital Skills and Training support available
  • Discuss the set-up of their work environment, following the health and safety guidance
  • Share the link to the University’s induction pages and ask them to review and complete the Making the most of your induction.
  • Work with your member of staff to plan the first couple of weeks of their working life at the University: what tasks do you expect them to complete and how will this work remotely? Who do you want them to speak to or meet with virtually?  Be clear about your expectations of them in terms of objectives, outputs and check-in points.
  • Over communicate – people are used to starting companies, meeting lots of people and being immersed into a social environment.  Schedule regular and frequent check-ins with them and make sure that they know how/when to get in touch with you. It’s important for new employees to become a functional part of the team; think about ways to remotely connect your new staff member with the rest of the team –this could be through virtual meetings, telephone conversations, shared projects.
  • Support them as they manage their wellbeing: starting a new role can be an exciting and daunting experience especially if they are not going to be based in their expected work environment. As with the rest of your team, follow the guidance for working from home and ensure that wellbeing is discussed in your regular check-ins.
  • Match them with a buddy who can check in with them every day and answer any informal questions. If you have a team member who has experience of working remotely this is ideal.
