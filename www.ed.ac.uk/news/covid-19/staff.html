Staff

General information explaining how the University will support and continue to pay staff during the Coronavirus outbreak, including guidance for those able to work from home and information on the University's use of the Coronavirus Job Retention Scheme, also known as the Furlough Scheme.

The following information explains how the University will support staff during the Coronavirus outbreak and its move to only 'essential services' on campus. For all staff, it covers self-isolation and practical guidance for those who are able to work from home. It elaborates on our commitment that staff will not suffer any financial detriment, i.e. loss of contractual pay and benefits, while the Government imposed physical and social distancing measures are in place.

Additional guidance is provided for managers. Staff are strongly encouraged to keep up to date by regularly reading this dedicated information on staff and employment matters, and the additional information on health, safety and travel.

Coronavirus Job Retention Scheme (the 'Furlough Scheme')

General information on the Job Retention Scheme (Updated 1 June)

As part of its response to the impact of Coronavirus (Covid-19) on the UK economy, the UK Government has implemented a temporary Coronavirus Job Retention Scheme. The Scheme is designed to help UK employers whose operations have been severely affected by Coronavirus and who consequently have no work for their employees. It helps them to retain and pay these employees rather than lay them off without pay or have to make them redundant. 

The Scheme currently allows employers to claim a grant from the UK Government to cover 80% of an employee’s pay, capped at £2,500 per month. The grant also covers the National Insurance contributions payable by the employer on this 80% or £2,500 and a minimal level of pension contributions.

While the Scheme can be used by all types of employers, those who fund their employees’ salaries and wages from public funds, i.e. by funds received from the UK or Scottish Governments, are much less likely to receive payment. The Scheme will close on 31 October  2020.

Note: changes to the Scheme were announced by the UK Chancellor on 27 May, to take effect from 1 July 2020. The implications of these changes for the University’s use of the scheme from this date will be reflected in these FAQs in due course.

What does ‘furlough’ mean?

To access the UK Government’s Job Retention Scheme, an employer has to ‘furlough’ the employees for whom it has no work. This means to place them on temporary leave albeit with pay. Employees must be furloughed for a minimum of three consecutive weeks and must not carry out any work at all while they are on furlough.  

The University's approach - who will be furloughed (Updated 1 June)

The University has decided that it will not access the Scheme for any member of staff whose salary is supported, in part or fully, by public funds, i.e. by funds received from the UK or Scottish Governments.

This means that it will not furlough any member of staff involved in the delivery of teaching, research or knowledge exchange activity, when those activities and support services are supported by NHS or UK Government grants or via the Scottish Funding Council, including the Research Excellence Grant. Such staff will continue to be paid as normal, with specific arrangements in place for those who are paid hourly, i.e. employed on Guaranteed Minimum Hours (GH) contracts.

Note: when the Scheme was first announced it was clear that posts funded by UK Research and Innovation (UKRI) could not be furloughed as they are supported by public funds. We are, however, now able to furlough UKRI funded researchers who are unable to work remotely and have secured an unfunded extension to their UKRI funded- project by 10th June 2020.    

We will not furlough anyone who is designated as an essential University of Edinburgh worker, and who will already have received an official letter confirming this.

We will furlough all other staff who are totally unable to work from home. While we can currently only claim back 80%, or £2,500 of an employee’s pay through the Scheme, we will continue to pay furloughed staff their full normal salary and contractual allowances. We will continue to deduct employee pension contributions as normal and to pay our employer contributions.

Information for furloughed staff (Updated 17 June)

If you are to be placed on furlough, your manager will contact you directly to discuss what this means for you. This will be followed up in writing. In summary: 

  • You will be asked to stay at home for at least three consecutive weeks, and potentially until at least 31 July  2020. If we need to reduce or extend the period of furlough your manager will be back in touch with you.
  • You must not undertake any work for the University during this period, but can be asked to do on-line training.
  • You can volunteer to help others outside the University during the crisis.
  • You will continue to accrue service with the University and annual leave while on furlough, although you may be asked to take some of your accrued leave at the end of the furlough period to ensure you have taken between 40% and 60% of it before mid-September 2020.
  • Please be reassured that being furloughed will have no bearing on your future career prospects or in any way signals that you would be at any more risk of being selected for redundancy in the future.  
  • Your manager will share more detailed information with you in this guidance for staff document which will continue to be updated in line with Government guidance and/or should the University’s approach change. 
  • We understand that this is a stressful time. Further information on wellbeing, advice and support services for staff is available in the FAQs below.

Employees with shielded status

If you have received a letter from the NHS advising you to take additional measures to protect yourself from the Coronavirus, please let your manager know. You can be furloughed if there is no work that you can do from home, and if your post is not publicly funded. If your post is publicly funded you will be placed on paid temporary leave.

Please note: you do not need to disclose medical information to your manager, you just need to advise them that you have received a shielding letter.

Employees with caring responsibilities

We recognise that many staff whose role can be done from home will also be juggling this with caring for children and/or other dependants. You may also be caring for a member of your household who is shielding.    

We have already asked, and continue to ask, that you do what you can in these circumstances and agree with your manager what is reasonable for you to do. However, if you find this impossible you can request to be furloughed – provided your role is not one that is publicly funded. 

You will need to bear in mind that furlough can be for a period of no less than three consecutive weeks – and that you cannot do any work at all. So you will need to discuss and agree this with your line manager in advance.

If furlough is not feasible, your manager will explore what adjustment(s), if any, can be made to your duties, priorities and deadlines, working hours and/or pattern to enable you to do some work while caring for your dependants. If none of this proves possible, and you cannot work at all, you will be placed on paid temporary leave.

Absence reporting

In light of the additional restrictions on social contact and the ‘lock-down’ imposed by the Scottish Government, everyone, other than those who have been designated by the University as ‘essential workers’ is at home and, if they can work from home, doing the best they can.   

Therefore we only need you to tell us if you are self-isolating if:

  • you develop symptoms of the virus, or 
  • a member of your household has developed symptoms and you have been designated by the University as an ‘essential worker’. 

In which case you should follow the usual absence reporting procedure for your School/Department. You must call your line manager or the designated contact in your School/Department to report your need to self-isolate.  Please make sure you are familiar with the reporting arrangements within your School/Department and have to hand the correct contact details.   

If you are well enough, i.e. have very mild symptoms, your manager will agree with you if you can work from home. This will depend on your symptoms and the nature of your role and whether you have the equipment, access and approval to do so.  Working from home for the period of isolation will have no effect on your pay, which will continue to be paid as normal.

If you are unable to work from home for any or all of the period of isolation i.e. because you are too unwell or you don’t have the equipment, access or approval to do your job from home - your manager will place you on paid leave.  Your absence will not be recorded as ordinary sickness and will have no effect on your absence record or entitlement to sick pay.

If you are self-isolating because you are unwell:

  • your absence will be treated as special sick leave
  • you will receive full pay for the period of absence related to the virus
  • your absence will have no effect on your absence record and entitlement to sick pay  

You must keep in regular contact with your manager, or the designated School/Department contact via email or telephone.  

As you are advised not to visit your GP, you may not be able to obtain a Fit Note. In these circumstances, the University will not require you to provide a Fit Note for any Coronavirus-related absence of 14 days or less.

Easter weekend rest break (Updated 1 June)

This year the University closed on  Friday 10 and  Monday 13 April to enable everyone working from home to switch off from work. Staff, other than those that have been designated by the University as ‘essential workers’, were not required to work on these days. 

The purpose of the two days of “collective annual leave” was for a simultaneous ‘pause’ in our working lives.  They were not taken from or added to anyone’s annual leave entitlement.

Essential staff, who were unable to take a break during this time, are able to take the equivalent of two of their working days at a later date, ideally as a long weekend, in agreement with their line-manager.

Carrying forward untaken leave

Our Annual Leave Policy allows staff to carry forward up to 5 days annual leave into the next holiday year, pro-rated for staff who work part-weeks.

The UK Government has relaxed the Working Time Regulations to allow workers to carry forward up to 20 days’ leave into the next two holiday years and 8 days into the next year. The intent behind this change being to allow key workers – e.g. those in the NHS and other industries, e.g. food distribution, who critical to the Covid-19 effort – to carry forward some or all of the leave that it was not reasonably practicable for them to take due to the outbreak.  

We very much hope that our designated essential workers (who have received an official letter to that effect to enable them to travel to and from work during the national ‘lock-down’) will be able to take 40-60% of their leave over the coming months – and we will encourage and support them to do so for their health and wellbeing. We will of course allow more than 5 days carry forward for any essential worker who has not been able to do so.

Access to University buildings and information for essential staff

Local building access (updated 29 June) 

At the moment access to campus for staff continues to be for ‘essential purpose’ only. Access to campus for ‘essential purpose’ will be managed by local management units who will confirm which of their staff can come on to campus for an ‘essential purpose’ which meets the criteria set out by Scottish Government. Local management units will retain a register of who has been approved to come on campus for an ‘essential purpose’ and will be responsible for making the necessary arrangements for individuals. This will include clear direction in the safety protocols which must be observed. This replaces the previous requirement to provide ‘essential worker’ letters to relevant staff and to maintain an institutional register of such staff.

Ensuring a safe working environment for essential staff

We have put procedures in place to ensure that essential staff accessing University buildings have a safe working environment. Staff should not attempt to come to work unless they have been identified as ‘essential staff’.

All identified essential staff must abide by the Government’s instructions. They must not come to work at all if they are in any of the vulnerable categories publicised or have any of the symptoms of Covid-19, no matter how mild.

Essential staff on site will abide by the following procedures to ensure their safety:

  • Rotas of a shorter duration have been introduced to maintain resilient teams
  • A buddy system will be followed as outlined by the University – lone working is not allowed
  • Social/physical distancing must be applied to all work activity, with a 2 metre minimum distance
  • Staff should wash hands frequently and clean regularly touched points such as door handles and light switches
  • All risk assessments for general work and lab work must be reviewed to take account of any changed methods of operating

Staff whose role can be done from home (Updated 1 June)

If your role can be done remotely, we do understand that you may be trying to do so while simultaneously caring for very young or disabled children, or supervising the home-schooling of older children. You may be doing this on your own and may also have other vulnerable or elderly dependants to care for.  If it is impossible for you to work from home due to your caring responsibilities, you should discuss this with your manager as you may be eligible for furlough, i.e. placed on temporary paid leave. More information on ‘furlough’ can be found above.

We know that what works for one member of staff working at home will not work for another. What worked in week one of the national lockdown may not work several weeks later, and we know that the needs of your household and dependants will change along the way, including as you perhaps move from home-schooling to what would have been school holidays.   

So there are no rules – it will be what works for you and we trust you to work that out and do what you can, when you can.  We’ll operate in the spirit of ‘best endeavors’. If you can work from home at some point in the day, we ask that you do so and that you do what you can to ensure your manager, your team and other key colleagues know when you are likely to be available. We understand that, with other members of the family at home, it may be difficult to predict when you will be available and that your availability to work may change at short notice.

 Working on-campus

I am in one of the vulnerable health groups, and I am intending to, or being asked, to return to work on-campus. What should I do? (Updated 26 June)

Extremely High Risk Group

If you are in the extremely high risk (shielding) group, you should have received a letter from NHS Scotland, or from your GP, and you are strongly advised to shield yourself to reduce your chance of getting coronavirus (Covid-19) and you must not return to work on campus. Please let your manager know. 

Higher Risk Group

If you are requesting to, or being asked to return to, work on campus and you have one of the conditions listed in the higher risk group, you should contact your GP for advice on any additional specific measures which need to be implemented to protect your health, as your GP has specific knowledge of your health. Please discuss any additional specific measures, as advised by your GP, with your line manager so that appropriate consideration can be given to reasonable adjustments which may be necessary to protect your health. 

Your manager may seek advice from the Occupational Health Service, via a management referral, if:

  1. Your GP is unable to provide advice
  2. You are unable to discuss your concerns with your line manager, or
  3. Either you or your manager require additional advice regarding your vulnerability to protect your health.

Thereafter, your manager will discuss with you any additional specific measures and/or adjustments, advised by your GP, which need to be implemented to protect your health and enable you to safely return to campus.  Your manager will discuss with you how to implement these where it is reasonable and appropriate to do to. This may include changes to work activities, practises or working patterns.

If the additional measures / adjustments needed to protect your health are unable to be implemented, your manager will ask you to stay at home.  If you are unable to work on campus and you are unable to do your work from home then you will remain on a period of temporary paid leave.

You do not need to disclose medical information to your manager, you just need to advise them that you are in one of the two vulnerable groups, and highlight any advice which you have been given by NHS / GP / Occupational Health to protect your health.

We have prepared a Covid-19 staff assessment form which you can use if you feel it is helpful, to let your manager know that you fall within one of the vulnerable health groups.  This form is not mandatory but available for those who wish to use it.

Health and wellbeing 

Wellbeing, advice and support services for staff

Feeling overwhemed or anxious by the ongoing Coronavirus outbreak is understandable. We appreciate that you may have concerns about how this will impact you or your family and friends.

Ways to Manage Fears & Anxieties:

  • Keep connected. Maintaining social networks, even online, can help maintain a sense of normality, and provide valuable outlets for sharing feelings and relieving stress.
  • Be mindful of assumptions about others. Someone who has a cough or a fever does not necessarily have coronavirus.  Self-awareness is important in not stigmatizing others in our community.
  • Stay healthy. Continue to practise good hygiene measures:
    • Avoid direct hand contact with your eyes, nose and mouth.
    • Wash hands with soap and water or alcohol hand sanitiser, after coughing or sneezing, after going to the toilet, and prior to eating and drinking.
    • When coughing or sneezing, cover your nose and mouth with disposable tissues and disposing of them in nearest waste bin after use, or cough or sneeze into your sleeve.
    • Avoid contact with others who are sick and stay home while sick.

Employment policies and other support 

Information for Guaranteed Minimum Hours staff

Staff on guaranteed minimum hours (GH) contracts will be paid as below during the Coronavirus outbreak whether or not they are placed on furlough, i.e. temporary paid leave:

April

Where staff have been allocated hours and are able to work from home (or on campus having been designated as an essential worker):

  • we will pay these allocated hours as normal, either through e-time or normal departmental processing to payroll.

Where staff have not been allocated hours, but would have expected to have been working were it not for Covid-19:

  • we will reflect the formula embedded in the Government’s Coronavirus Job Retention Scheme, which means you will receive the higher amount of either a) the pay for April last year or b) the average pay in the 2019/20 tax year, and this will be paid at 100%.
  • however, given the rapid transition to remote working, unfortunately we were not able to finalise these changes by the April payroll cut-off date.
  • therefore, to ensure this group of staff are paid in April, we propose to re-pay the hours processed for payment in February 2020. We see this as fairer to staff, as it is a more representative month than March given the period of strike action which fell at the end of February and early March.
  • we will however undertake a reconciliation and pay any shortfall in May. We will not recover any overpayment.

Staff who would not have been offered any hours in other circumstances will not be working and, as normal, will therefore not be paid.

May onwards 

Where staff have been allocated hours and are able to work from home (or on campus having been designated as an essential worker):

  • we will pay these allocated hours as normal, either through e-time or normal departmental processing to payroll.

Where staff have been allocated and scheduled hours but are unable to work from home:

  • we will pay what was allocated/scheduled, either through e-time or normal departmental processing to payroll.

Where staff have not been allocated hours, but would have expected to have been were it not for Covid-19:

  • we will pay the higher of last May’s pay and the 2019/20 tax year average (again at 100%)

Staff who would not have been offered any hours in other circumstances will not be working and, as normal, will therefore not be paid.

Coronavirus Job Retention Scheme (the 'Furlough Scheme') (Updated 1 June)

General information and summary of University approach

As outlined in the staff FAQs above, the Coronavirus Job Retention Scheme is designed to help businesses whose operations are directly and adversely impacted by the Coronavirus (Covid-19) pandemic to the extent that they have no work for some or all of their employees and consequently would have to lay them off or make them redundant.

The University has decided that it will not furlough staff who hold posts which are publicly funded, in full or in part. While there was originally  a degree of uncertainty regarding the approach being taken by UK Research and Innovation (UKRI), the University is now, now able to furlough UKRI funded researchers who are unable to work remotely and have secured an unfunded extension to their UKRI funded- project by 10th June 2020.

It has also decided to take a prudent approach to the inclusion of staff on fixed-term posts. While Government guidance suggests that staff on fixed-term contracts can be furloughed and their contract renewed during the period of furlough, in line with the underlying ethos of the Scheme, this should be directly attributable to the impact of Covid-19. 

Consequently, fixed-term contracts will be renewed on a case by case basis, reflecting the business critical nature of the work involved. This will include where the role is critical to our Covid-19 recovery activity and/or existing plans which continue to be of strategic priority and for which there is budget.

Selection of staff to be placed on furlough   

Heads of HR and HR Partners will work with senior managers to identify and agree which employees can be placed on furlough. If there is work for some but not all of the same role-holders, staff can be rotated on and off of work/furlough, provided they are each furloughed for a continuous period of three weeks. As in all matters, due consideration must be taken to ensure selection decisions are fair and do not discriminate against any cohort of staff.

Once identified, proposals will be collated and presented to the Head of College/Professional Services Group for sign-off before being approved by the University’s Senior Leadership Team.

Decision making

The starting point for all furlough decision making is whether there is any work a staff member can do while at home. If there is no work, the second key question is whether their salary is covered by public funds to any degree. If there is no work and their salary is not covered by public funds, then they can be furloughed. This ‘twin’ test also applies to staff who are shielding. Staff with caring responsibilities can be furloughed even if there is work for them to do, provided their salary is not publicly funded.

Furlough process for June onwards (Updated 1 June)

I have staff on furlough and I need to return them to work. How do I do this?

In line with the rules of the CJRS, employees must be placed on furlough for a minimum of three consecutive weeks.  If for some reason you need to recall a staff member from furlough within the first three weeks, you must speak to your HR Advisor or Partner before doing so.

In all circumstances, the line manager or department manager should contact the member of staff and agree the date that furlough will end and when they will return to work.  Appropriate notice to allow for any caring responsibilities should be discussed and considered, as well as any concerns staff members may have. 

To take the member of staff off of furlough, you must inform HR using the template which can be found here. Please ensure contact details are correct prior to sending to HR to avoid any delays. 

HR will then provide written confirmation to the email address provided previously, or by letter to the staff member’s home address if required.  The named manager will be copied in to the email sent to the colleague. Please note, this will normally be within 7 calendar days, unless otherwise advised.

Staff should not return to work from furlough without receiving this confirmation.

How do I place staff on furlough?

Managers must have the conversation directly with staff and agree the date that furlough will begin. In line with recently announced changes by the UK Government, this cannot be any later than 10th June 2020.

Your HR Advisor or Partner can support you with guidance for this conversation. ​​​Once this conversation has taken place, you will need to inform HR Services using the template here. 

HR will then provide written confirmation to the email address provided. Please ensure contact details are checked with the staff member prior to sending to HR to avoid any delays. 

What is happening with staff currently on furlough until 30th June, will they be extended?

Staff can remain on furlough until 31st July 2020.  Although the CJRS will continue beyond this date, the rules are changing from 1st July and we are not currently clear what this will mean for the University.  As soon as we can, we will share more detail on the longer term for furlough.

Unless you tell us otherwise, your staff members currently on furlough will be automatically extended to 31st July 2020 by the HR department and confirmation will be sent to them directly.

I need to bring staff back from furlough on the current end date of 30th June, what should I do?

You should follow the process for returning staff to work by having the conversation with the staff member, and agreeing an appropriate notice period and date to return.  You should then notify HR using the template here. 

HR will then provide written confirmation to the email address provided previously, or by letter to the staff member’s home address if required.  The named manager will be copied in to the email sent to the colleague.  Please note, this will normally be within 7 calendar days, unless otherwise advised.

Staff should not return to work from furlough without receiving this confirmation.

Easter weekend rest break

As referenced in the above section on ‘Annual leave’, the University  closed from Friday 10 to Monday 13 April to enable everyone currently at work to switch off from work. The purpose of this “collective annual leave” was to allow a simultaneous ‘pause’ in all of our working lives.   

We want to keep things simple and as administratively light as we can for all concerned - and everyone is continuing to be paid.

  • Managers do not need to record these days as annual leave. This applies whether or not staff are able to work remotely.
  • To ensure annual leave balances are correct however, staff who had already booked annual leave to cover Good Friday and Easter Monday should follow their normal School/Department arrangements and cancel their booking. They do not need to replace these with the rest day(s). If feasible, departments should look at how best to collectively remove pre-booked leave from their team records on behalf of individual staff.
  • As these respite days were for staff currently in our employment, we won’t be adding the two days to the annual leave calculator. Nor will be they be added for staff currently on long-term leave, e.g. maternity leave, sick leave.

Staff designated as ‘essential workers’ (who will have received an official letter from the University to that effect to enable them to travel to and from work during the national ‘lock-down’) who were unable to take a break at this time will be able to take the time at a later date, ideally as a long weekend, as agreed with their line-manager.

If any other member of staff was unable to take these days off due to business critical activities which could not be postponed, they must agree with their line manager which days they will designate as respite days in the fortnight ending 24 April. Wherever possible, they should be encouraged to pattern this to give themselves a ‘long weekend’. 

Guaranteed hours (GH) staff scheduled to work on Friday 10 and Monday 13 April will be paid for the hours they were scheduled to work. If they were required to work, every effort should be made to provide equivalent respite in the following fortnight, i.e. by 24 April, when the same number of hours should again be processed for payment. If respite time is not feasible, e.g. because no work is available until later, or at all, the same number of hours as worked on 10 and 13 April should again be processed for payment.  

Managing staff who work from home (Updated 17 July) 

Detailed guidance for managers on home, remote and hybrid working is now available.

These include information on managing staff with caring responsibilities, expenses and digital skills training and resources.

Managing staff with caring responsibilities

It is highly likely that a number of your staff will be trying to work from home while simultaneously caring for their children and other dependants. Some will have support, others will not. Some will be sharing their home as a workplace, including IT. Consequently, they will not be, and cannot be expected to be, anywhere near as productive as they were on campus.

Managers must take all of this into account when assigning and following-up on work, and their staff can expect them to take a sympathetic, ‘best endeavours’ and flexible approach during these unprecedented times. 

If adjustments to duties, priorities and deadlines, working hours and/or pattern (and a combination thereof) are insufficient in helping staff to do some work and care for their dependants, they must be placed on paid temporary leave. Managers must seek advice from their HR Advisor on whether the staff member can be placed on ‘furlough’. 

You must agree with your staff member how best to keep in touch with them while they are on leave. As and when the national restrictions ease, and the University re-opens its buildings, you should discuss with them how best they can return to work.   

To ease the administrative burden on all concerned, no flexible working requests need to be made or recorded. The temporary leave does not need to be recorded in the HR System or any local ‘absence’ recording systems.

Expenses

The University will exceptionally consider meeting the purchase cost of equipment or capability only where it is “wholly, exclusively and necessary” to homeworking and could not be supplied in advance by the University.

Line managers must carefully consider the expense claims that they receive, to ensure that they are necessary for home working during this temporary situation. For expense claims over £100, or where the claim was not authorised in advance, or the line manager is uncertain on whether the claim is necessary to home working, then they should consult with their local Finance or IT Manager before approving. Schools and departments may require additional local checks on this expenditure.

Honouring Job Offers

All job offers (verbal or written) already made prior to our move to remote working should have been  honoured. Every effort must me l be made to ensure the best on-boarding and induction experience for new staff. 

Recruitment Activity prior to Offer

If you are in the process of recruiting but have not yet reached offer stage, i.e. have already advertised the post and are actively long/short listing etc, you are encouraged to seriously consider the implications and practicalities of proceeding with an appointment at this time.  

Options may be to:

  • Pause – communicating with candidates to let them know when t the process will restart the process
  • Cancel – if there is the potential that resource needs might be changed as a consequence of the pandemic.
  • Complete appointment – if the appointment is business critical and you have confirmed that with your budget holder.

Recruitment not yet started

If you have a vacancy but have not yet advertised the post, it is likely that the pool of applicants will be significantly depleted, particularly if applicants would need to relocate to Edinburgh.

You should therefore seriously consider delaying what might not prove to be a productive exercise. 

We should however progress business critical appointments that will support recovery post Covid-19. Line managers and the Main Budget Holders normal approval processes should be followed.   

International recruits (Updated 10 June) 

During the Coronavirus (Covid-19) situation, there are likely to be restrictions on a new recruit’s ability to travel to the UK to take up their post.

Managers must not assume that non-UK nationals - unable to travel to the UK to take up their post - can work remotely, i.e. from their home country, or the country in which they are currently located.  Working overseas for a UK employer – even for a short period of time - can have immigration/work-permit, employment law, tax and social security implications (for both the individual and the University) in that third-country. These implications must be fully explored and risk assessed before any ‘home working’ agreement is put in place.

Managers must therefore seek early advice from their HR Advisor to ensure appropriate specialist advice is obtained to inform such an agreement.  

Please note that your new recruit will need to self-isolate for 14 days if they enter the UK on or after Monday 8th June 2020. You will therefore need to manage their induction remotely and decide if they can undertake any work from home.

New recruits and remote induction

With traditional working arrangements rapidly changing due to the impact of the Covid-19 virus here is some guidance on how to support the induction of new staff when working remotely.

  • Speak to the member of staff as soon as possible before their start date to update them on the University’s guidance regarding Covid-19  and to answer any questions and concerns they may have. Discuss and agree how you will communicate with each other and the team.
  • Review the Induction Guidance for Managers to plan a successful induction considering how to adapt best practice for a remote working environment based on their role.
  • If it is possible for the staff member to work from home, review and discuss with them the Guidance for working from home on the Covid-19 webpages
  • Support them to set up and work remotely. They may not be familiar with the tools the University has for keeping in touch, give them plenty of time to get themselves set up and test connectivity.
  • Make them aware of the Digital Skills and Training support available
  • Discuss the set-up of their work environment, following the health and safety guidance
  • Share the link to the University’s induction pages and ask them to review and complete the Making the most of your induction.
  • Work with your member of staff to plan the first couple of weeks of their working life at the University: what tasks do you expect them to complete and how will this work remotely? Who do you want them to speak to or meet with virtually?  Be clear about your expectations of them in terms of objectives, outputs and check-in points.
  • Over communicate – people are used to starting companies, meeting lots of people and being immersed into a social environment.  Schedule regular and frequent check-ins with them and make sure that they know how/when to get in touch with you. It’s important for new employees to become a functional part of the team; think about ways to remotely connect your new staff member with the rest of the team –this could be through virtual meetings, telephone conversations, shared projects.
  • Support them as they manage their wellbeing: starting a new role can be an exciting and daunting experience especially if they are not going to be based in their expected work environment. As with the rest of your team, follow the guidance for working from home and ensure that wellbeing is discussed in your regular check-ins.
  • Match them with a buddy who can check in with them every day and answer any informal questions. If you have a team member who has experience of working remotely this is ideal.
